As fresh water quality deteriorates there is great need to reduce nutrient inputs to watersheds.

This problems is usually addressed via the reductions of Phosphorous (P), based on the fact that Phosphorous is a harder to come-by limiting nutrient and thus when available algal blooms take full advantage of it.

These blooms harm the water quality and biodiversity therein.

However in their paper, Paerl et al. argue that reducing only P is not enough and that the best solution to prevent the eutrophication of lakes and downstream ecosystems is to reduce both Phosphorous and Nitrogen (N).

Harmful algal blooms (or HABs) are impacting some important lakes around the world - these lakes show a cycle when it comes to nutrient loads patterns foretelling of a combined P and N effect to the respective lakes biomass production.

N fertilizer use in the form of urea has dramatically increased and surpassed ammonium nitrate since 1984:

Measures need to be taken by the agencies that care for our waters to measure and reduce for both N and P inputs.
